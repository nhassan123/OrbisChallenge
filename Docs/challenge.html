Scoring and Tournament¶

Below, you can find information on how to do well in the Challenge!

1. Grading Scheme¶

Very simply, each team earns 1 point per tile owned at the end of the game (300 turns).

2. Submissions¶

Follow the below steps when you are ready to submit your AI.

  1. Zip up the AI code (just your AI, not the game libraries!)

Warning

Upon submission, your zip file is renamed with the naming convention “team_name.zip”. Make sure you don’t make any absolute file references in your code that may be impacted by folder names!

Compatible Python Libraries

We have installed the following non-standard python libraries on our grading machine. All other libraries, if you choose to use them, must be zipped up with the bot on your submission.

  • Numpy
  • Matplotlib
  • Tensorflow
  • Pytorch
  • Pandas
  1. Upload your newly created archives on ‘Submit your AI’ on your dashboard on the Orbis Challenge website. If you are working in a team, only one member has to upload it.

Resubmissions and Versions

You’re welcome to submit more than once! Previous submissions of your AI are also available for download on the submission portal.

3. Live Leaderboard and Competition¶

Throughout the tournament, a live leaderboard will track your place amongst competitors (provided that you made a submission). We’re constantly simulating games with the latest submissions of competitors’ AIs, and the Glicko-2 system is used to place these AIs on the leaderboard. Note that the precise rank of Top 5 competitors are not disclosed.

Live Leaderboard vs. Final Rank

Live leaderboard ranks are not final! After the conclusion of the tournament, we simulate more games to ensure that our rankings are accurate. However, it should give a good idea of your standing amongst Orbis Challenge competitors.

At the conclusion of the tournament, we run many more games to accurately determine Top 20 AIs from submissions. These Top 20 AIs are holistically evaluated by both performance and code review to narrow down the Top 5 AIs. Winners get to give a short presentation about their AI!

4. Viewing Recent Matches¶

Throughout the tournament, you can also check out recent matches on the ‘View Recent Matches’ tab on your dashboard. This contains information about which AI you faced off against, final scores, as well as the turns that all AIs spent disabled. Logs from these games are also available for download, in case you want to take a closer look at your AI’s performance.

5. Game Settings¶

All games will be played using the following settings:

  • Board is symmetrical, and its size is set at 30 x 30.
  • Maximum amount of time allowed for your AI to make a move is 600 milliseconds.

6. Grading in Detail¶

As the game is a 4 player game, grading is not as simple as having 1 winner each game! We follow the below steps to tune the Glicko-2 rating of each AI after each simulation.

  1. Retrieve results from the game (scores of each AI).
  2. Derive a pair-wise game matrix for all four AIs involved.
  3. Re-evaluate Glicko-2 rating based on the pair-wise performance of each AI.

Example Game

For instance, consider the following outcome of a game - A: 100, B: 75, C: 50, D: 25. We transform these game results into a pair-wise matrix as follows.

  A B C D
A
L L L
B W
L L
C W W
L
D W W W

Essentially, each game turns into 3 games for each player, against every other opponent in the game. This way, the second place AI is still rewarded for its relatively good performance, while the last place AI is punished for its underperformance.

7. Code Review¶

This is the part where we evaluate your code! Your AI has proven that it can perform, but how well-coded is it? Be sure to make your code as readable as possible and leave some comments for the code reviewers to read!

8. Presentation¶

The teams that have their submissions land in the top 5 after the playoffs and code review are asked to give a short presentation to talk about their key strategies, the problems they faced and how they overcame them.
